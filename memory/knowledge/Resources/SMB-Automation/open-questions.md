# Open Questions: Known Unknowns in SMB Automation

## 1. Can AI-native tools displace established SMB software, or will incumbents adapt?

**The question**: Tools like Craft, Clay, and various AI-powered SMB assistants promise to automate workflows that established tools handle manually. Will they create new categories or simply be absorbed by incumbents?

**Why it's hard to answer**: 
- Incumbents have distribution, brand trust, and integration ecosystems
- SMB buyers are notoriously slow to switch (switching costs feel high even when they aren't)
- The "AI" label has been overused, making buyers skeptical
- The real value may be in augmentation, not replacement

**What would help**: Data on AI-native SMB tool adoption curves, and whether any have crossed 100K paying SMB customer thresholds.

---

## 2. What is the sustainable CAC/LTV ratio for SMB SaaS at scale?

**The question**: Many SMB SaaS companies grew fast with high CAC (customer acquisition cost) and low ARPU (average revenue per user), relying on venture capital to cover the gap. What are the unit economics that actually work long-term?

**Why it's hard to answer**:
- Public data on SMB SaaS unit economics is sparse (companies don't disclose this granularly)
- Different verticals have wildly different LTVs (a dental practice pays more than a freelance blogger)
- The rise of free alternatives (Notion, Google, Slack) has compressed pricing across categories
- Churn rates vary enormously by category and depend on product depth

**What would help**: More founders sharing real numbers, especially post-downturn. Most published metrics are from growth-phase companies, not mature businesses.

---

## 3. Will vertical-specific solutions beat horizontal platforms in SMB, or will horizontal winners integrate verticals?

**The question**: Companies like ServiceTitan (construction), Toast (restaurants), and Mindbody (wellness) own specific verticals. Companies like QuickBooks and Salesforce try to be horizontal. Which model creates more durable value?

**Why it's hard to answer**:
- Verticals have higher margins but smaller total addressable markets
- Horizontals can theoretically expand into verticals via acquisition, but integration often fails
- Platform shifts (mobile, cloud, AI) can disrupt both models
- The "buy vs. build" decision for SMBs keeps shifting

**What would help**: Tracking which vertical players successfully IPO or get acquired at premium valuations vs. horizontal players, and analyzing the integration success rates after M&A.
