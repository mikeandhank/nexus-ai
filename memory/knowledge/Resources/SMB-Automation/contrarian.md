# Contrarian Insights: What People Get Wrong About Serving SMBs

## Conventional Wisdom vs. Reality

### 1. "SMBs Want Cheap, Basic Tools"
**Wrong.** SMBs want tools that *work*, not bare-bones versions of enterprise software. They resent being treated as "small fish" with gimped features. The real insight: SMBs will pay premium prices for solutions that solve their specific problems, but they demand parity with enterprise functionality.

**Why it fails**: Building "SMB versions" with intentional limitations creates resentment and positions your product as second-class.

---

### 2. "Self-Serve Works for Everyone"
**Wrong.** While product-led growth works for some SMBs, many segments (medical practices, law firms, contractors) need human hand-holding. The assumption that SMBs prefer zero-touch onboarding ignores the reality that many owners are technophobic and overwhelmed.

**Why it fails**: Over-reliance on self-serve creates high churn among segments that need implementation support. The 70% of SMBs who abandon tools after free trials often do so because they couldn't get unstuck.

---

### 3. " SMBs Are All Alike"
**Wrong.** A freelance accountant has almost nothing in common with a manufacturing shop or a restaurant. Treating "SMB" as a single segment leads to feature bloat that serves no one well.

**Why it fails**: Generic positioning leads to weak product-market fit. The most successful SMB tools verticalize deeply.

---

### 4. "Network Effects Will Drive Adoption"
**Wrong.** Unlike B2B enterprise, SMBs don't have extensive internal networks to trigger network effects. The "sticky" assumption ignores how quickly SMBs will abandon tools that don't deliver immediate value.

**Why it fails**: SMBs have low switching costs and no organizational inertia keeping them in place. What feels "networked" to the seller feels like lock-in to the buyer.

---

### 5. "Freemium Drives Growth"
**Wrong.** Freemium works when the free tier creates real value that naturally upgrades. But SMBs on free plans often represent false positives—they'll never pay and consume support resources. The real metric is conversion velocity, not user count.

**Why it fails**: Freemium creates an inverted funnel: lots of users, few converters, high support costs. Many SMB freemium experiments are just expensive lead generation.

---

### 6. "SMBs Want AI"
**Wrong.** They want solutions to their problems. AI as a feature doesn't sell—outcomes do. The current wave of "AI-powered" SMB tools often adds complexity without solving core pain points.

**Why it fails**: SMBs don't care about the technology stack. They care that their invoicing gets done, their inventory is tracked, their customers are contacted. AI marketing often obscures real value.

---

### 7. "Build It and They Will Come"
**Wrong.** The "if we build great features, SMBs will find us" mindset ignores the extremely fragmented attention of the SMB buyer. They're overwhelmed with options and rely heavily on peer recommendations, review sites, and trade publications.

**Why it fails**: Product quality alone doesn't break through. Distribution and trust-building matter as much as the product.

---

### 8. "Enterprise Sales Tactics Work on SMBs"
**Wrong.** Long sales cycles, enterprise pricing tables, and demo-first approaches repel SMB buyers who want to sign up in 5 minutes and get immediate value.

**Why it fails**: SMBs make purchase decisions differently. They're not buying based on roadmaps and QBRs—they're buying based on immediate need and trust.

---

## The Real Insights

1. **Vertical beats horizontal** - The most durable SMB companies own specific verticals (construction, dental, restaurants) rather than trying to be everything to everyone.

2. **Time-to-value is the metric** - Not feature count, not price. How fast can you solve a specific problem?

3. **Support is a feature** - The "no support" model fails for SMBs who need human help when stuck.

4. **Community is Moat** - Building user communities creates switching costs and organic growth that pure product can't match.

5. **Revenue per user > User count** - A small number of paying users beats a large number of free users.
