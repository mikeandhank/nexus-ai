# Consumer Products & Marketplaces — Case Studies

## 5 Durable Successes

### 1. Amazon Marketplace (2000s-present)

**What happened:** From online bookstore to dominant third-party marketplace (now 50%+ of unit sales).

**Core insight:** 
- Fulfillment infrastructure is competitive moat (FBA)
- Network effects on both sides are self-reinforcing
- Trust mechanisms (reviews, A-to-Z guarantee) reduce friction

**Fatal assumption they avoided:** That being a marketplace doesn't require infrastructure investment.

**What it teaches:** You can build a platform without owning inventory, but you still need to build infrastructure (logistics, trust, payments). The marketplace that invests in fulfillment wins.

---

### 2. Shopify (2006-present)

**What happened:** From small e-commerce tool to infrastructure layer for e-commerce.

**Core insight:** 
- Enable sellers, don't compete with them
- Ecosystem of apps creates lock-in
- Merchant success = platform success

**Fatal assumption they avoided:** That e-commerce requires being a retailer.

**What it teaches:** The infrastructure play can be more valuable than the direct play. Rather than competing with merchants, power them.

---

### 3. Airbnb (2008-present)

**What happened:** From air mattresses to $100B+ accommodation marketplace.

**Core insight:** 
- Seeded supply with photographer program and early host recruitment
- Built trust through review system (visible to both sides)
- Created new category by educating both sides

**Fatal assumption they avoided:** That supply will appear without investment.

**What it teaches:** In two-sided markets, you must subsidize one side (usually supply) to get started. Quality > quantity in early days.

---

### 4. Warby Parker (2010-present)

**What happened:** From DTC disruptor to omnichannel brand (230+ stores, profitable).

**Core insight:** 
- Word-of-mouth > paid acquisition (retention > new customer costs)
- Physical retail as customer acquisition channel, not just revenue
- Diversified beyond glasses into contacts, eye exams

**Fatal assumption they avoided:** That DTC means digital-only.

**What it teaches:** Pure digital DTC has limits. Physical retail can reduce CAC when digital acquisition costs rise. The channel strategy is the product strategy.

---

### 5. Etsy (2005-present)

**What happened:** Niche marketplace for handmade goods that survived Amazon's competition.

**Core insight:** 
- Specialized beats general
- Community drives engagement and trust
- Curated experience maintains quality

**Fatal assumption they avoided:** That scale requires becoming Amazon.

**What it teaches:** Niche focus creates defensibility. Amazon can't beat you at being specialized. Community is a moat — it's hard to replicate culture.

## 5 Instructive Failures

### 1. Quibi (2020)

**What happened:** Short-form video streaming with $1.75B in funding, failed within 6 months.

**Fatal assumption:** 
- Mobile-first short-form video was an untapped market
- Users would pay for content they could get elsewhere for free

**What went wrong:**
- No cultural moment or shared experience (no "watercooler" effect)
- Content wasn't differentiated from TikTok/YouTube
- Launched during COVID when mobile-only made less sense

**What it teaches:** Funding doesn't create market fit. Differentiation matters. Content platforms need either uniqueness or community, not just production quality.

---

### 2. Webvan (1999-2001)

**What happened:** Online grocery delivery — $830M raised, burned through it in 2 years, IPO → bankruptcy.

**Fatal assumption:** 
- Could build logistics infrastructure from scratch
- Scale would solve unit economics
- Consumer behavior was ready for online grocery

**What went wrong:**
- Premature national expansion (28 cities before proving model)
- Massive infrastructure costs (warehouses, trucks, technology)
- Customer acquisition costs exceeded lifetime value

**What it teaches:** Logistics is extremely hard. Prove unit economics in one market before expanding. Don't confuse growth with progress.

---

### 3. Allbirds (2015-present, struggles since 2021)

**What happened:** Sustainable footwear DTC, IPO in 2021, now closing stores and pivoting.

**Fatal assumption:** 
- Sustainability alone creates differentiation
- DTC economics work at scale without physical retail

**What went wrong:**
- Product seen as undifferentiated (sneakers are sneakers)
- Physical expansion proved harder than expected
- Profitability elusive despite growth

**What it teaches:** Differentiation must matter to the customer, not just the founder. DTC is a go-to-market strategy, not a business model. Physical retail can be necessary even for digital-born brands.

---

### 4. Karhoo (2016-2017)

**What happened:** Ride-sharing aggregator — raised $10-50M, burned through it fighting Uber/Lyft.

**Fatal assumption:** 
- Could aggregate supply without owning it
- Aggregator model works in ride-sharing

**What went wrong:**
- Driver quality inconsistent (couldn't control experience)
- Uber/Lyft had superior economics and supply
- No differentiation in a winner-take-most market

**What it teaches:** In markets with strong network effects, aggregator plays often fail. You need control over supply or a defensible niche. Competing on "we're not the incumbent" isn't enough.

---

### 5. PepperTap (2014-2016)

**What happened:** Indian grocery delivery — raised $50M+, shut down.

**Fatal assumption:** 
- Funding solves execution
- Indian market ready for online grocery

**What went wrong:**
- High delivery fees made it a premium service
- Poor product experience
- Unit economics never worked — couldn't sustain delivery costs
- Competition from bigger players (Grofers, BigBasket)

**What it teaches:** Funding amplifies everything, including mistakes. Unit economics matter in every market. Being well-funded isn't a strategy.

---

## Summary: What Separates Winners from Losers

| Factor | Success | Failure |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Distribution | Infrastructure-first | Product-first |
| Growth | Profitable first | Scale first |
| Trust | Engineered explicitly | Assumed buildable |
| Competition | Niche/defensible | Generalist/direct |
| Capital | Proves unit economics | Replaces execution |
| Physical retail | Omnichannel when needed | Digital-only rigidity |
