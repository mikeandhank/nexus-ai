# Consumer Products & Marketplaces — Open Questions

## Known Unknowns

### 1. How do you profitably win against dominant platforms?

**The tension:** Amazon/eBay have massive infrastructure advantages, network effects, and resources. Yet niche players (Etsy, Wayfair) have survived and even thrived. The question is whether you can build a *durable* business, not just a lifestyle company.

**Why I can't answer confidently:**
- Amazon's moat compounds (logistics → lower costs → more buyers → more sellers)
- Network effects create winner-take-most dynamics
- Resources and data advantages are hard to overcome
- Competition from China (Shein, Temu) changes the economics entirely

**What would change my mind:**
- Case studies of niche marketplaces that survived Amazon's expansion
- Data on category defensibility (which categories are safe?)
- New technology shifts (AI, blockchain) that level the playing field
- Geographic or regulatory moats that actually work

**My current hypothesis:** Niche verticalization is the only viable path, and even then, exit may be more likely than IPO.

---

### 2. What's the optimal balance between marketplace fees and seller retention?

**The tension:** High fees sustain the platform but drive sellers to alternatives. Low fees attract sellers but may not cover customer acquisition and trust infrastructure. Amazon charges high fees but sellers stay for volume — but can a new entrant replicate that?

**Why I can't answer confidently:**
- Amazon's fees are high, but volume justifies it
- Lower fees attract sellers but can attract the wrong kind (low-margin, high-volume)
- Seller economics vary wildly by category
- The threat of disintermediation (direct sales, other platforms) is real

**What would change my mind:**
- Detailed seller economics by category
- Case studies of fee changes and seller churn
- Research on what sellers actually optimize for (volume, fees, ease of use)
- Analysis of multi-platform selling behavior

**My current hypothesis:** Fee structure matters less than seller success. The platform that makes sellers money wins, regardless of fee level.

---

### 3. Is DTC (Direct-to-Consumer) a viable long-term strategy?

**The tension:** DTC promised to cut out the middleman and capture margin. Warby Parker proved it can work — but as an omnichannel brand, not purely digital. Many pure DTC brands (Allbirds, Casper, others) struggled post-IPO.

**Why I can't answer confidently:**
- Customer acquisition costs rose 50-100%+ in 2020-2024
- Physical retail is expensive to build but may be necessary
- Brand differentiation is hard to maintain without continuous spending
- The "digital-first" narrative may have been a fase

**What would change my mind:**
- Analysis of DTC brands that achieved sustained profitability without wholesale
- Data on CAC trends and whether they're reversible
- Case studies of successful DTC → omnichannel transitions
- Evidence that brand alone drives retention without ongoing spend

**My current hypothesis:** Pure DTC is rarely a complete business model — it's a go-to-market strategy that often requires wholesale or physical retail to reach scale profitably.

---

### 4. How will AI reshape consumer marketplaces?

**The tension:** AI can potentially solve matching, personalization, fraud detection, and customer service. But it could also commoditize differentiation, empower competitors faster, and shift power to those with data advantages.

**Why I can't answer confidently:**
- We're too early to see durable patterns
- AI could either concentrate power (data moats) or democratize it (tools for small sellers)
- The "AI native" marketplace hasn't been proven yet
- Regulation is an unknown variable

**What would change my mind:**
- Evidence of AI-native marketplaces gaining traction
- Data on AI's impact on seller success and buyer experience
- Case studies of platforms that successfully integrated AI
- Understanding of which tasks AI automates vs. augments

**My current hypothesis:** AI will make good marketplaces better but won't solve fundamental problems (trust, distribution, chicken-and-egg). The platform with the best data wins — and existing leaders have a data advantage.

## Priority Research Areas

1. **Niche defensibility** — Which categories can survive Amazon?
2. **Seller economics** — What makes sellers stay vs. leave?
3. **DTC sustainability** — Is omnichannel required for durability?
4. **AI disruption** — How do new technologies change the game?

---

## Note on Confidence

These questions are "known unknowns" — the domain has patterns, but the answers depend on factors outside the model (execution, timing, capital, luck). The goal isn't certainty; it's knowing where to place bets and what to watch.
