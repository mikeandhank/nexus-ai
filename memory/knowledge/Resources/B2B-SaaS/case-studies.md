# B2B SaaS — Case Studies

## 5 Durable Successes

### 1. Salesforce

**What happened:** Built the CRM category from nothing to $35B+ revenue, $226B+ market cap.

**Core insight:** 
- Landed with small businesses, expanded upmarket systematically
- Created ecosystem (AppExchange) for defensibility — 6,000+ apps
- Subscription model → predictable recurring revenue
- PaaS play turned it into platform, not just software

**Fatal assumption (that others make):** CRM is a feature set you can compete on.

**What it teaches:** 
- Category creation is possible
- Partner ecosystems create moats larger than product
- Land and expand works — but requires product depth at each tier

---

### 2. Slack (Salesforce acquisition, $27B)

**What happened:** From gaming company pivot (Tiny Speck) to fastest enterprise app adoption in history.

**Core insight:**
- Product-led growth before PLG was a term
- Network effects — each new user makes it more valuable (workplace coordination)
- Bottom-up adoption (teams, not executives) — freed team from IT gatekeepers
- Product as the entire GTM motion — virality built in

**Fatal assumption (that others make):** Enterprise software requires enterprise sales.

**What it teaches:**
- Bottom-up can beat top-down
- Network effects are powerful moats
- Product as the entire GTM motion works if product is exceptional
- Sometimes the acquirer is your exit (and that's fine)

---

### 3. HubSpot

**What happened:** Built inbound marketing category, went public, $25B+ valuation.

**Core insight:**
- Created the "inbound marketing" methodology — owned the category
- Content/SEO as primary distribution — 50M+ monthly blog visitors
- Freemium model → SMB market → land small, expand up
- Productized services angle: provides the tools that embody the methodology

**Fatal assumption (that others make):** You need a sales team to sell B2B.

**What it teaches:**
- Content can be distribution (but requires patience)
- Methodology creation builds category ownership
- SMB can sustain massive SaaS business
- Align product with the methodology you teach

---

### 4. Stripe

**What happened:** Became payments infrastructure for internet businesses, valued $95B+.

**Core insight:**
- Developer experience as differentiator — docs > marketing
- Embedded financial infrastructure — you use Stripe without knowing it
- Expand from payments to broader financial services (Stripe Connect, Radar, Capital)
- Network effect: more merchants → more processors → better rates

**Fatal assumption (that others make):** Payments is a commodity business.

**What it teaches:**
- Developer experience is a moat (requires culture, not just resources)
- Infrastructure businesses can be category-defining
- Expand horizontally once anchored — but only after deep vertical integration
- The "boring" businesses can be the most valuable

---

### 5. Datadog

**What happened:** Cloud monitoring platform, $44B+ market cap, grew through cloud transformation.

**Core insight:**
- Unified monitoring across cloud (AWS → GCP → Azure) — solved a real pain
- Product-led: free trials, self-serve, developer adoption
- Expansion: APM → log management → security → observability
- Landed with dev teams, expanded to CTO / CISO

**Fatal assumption (that others make):** Monitoring is a feature, not a platform.

**What it teaches:**
- Unify what was fragmented — that's the play
- Developer tool adoption precedes enterprise adoption
- Expand to adjacent categories once established
- Cloud transition created massive TAM

## 5 Instructive Failures

### 1. WeWork

**What happened:** $47B valuation → Chapter 11 bankruptcy, now ~$15B.

**Fatal assumption:** 
- Could scale real estate like software
- Valuation justified by "technology company" narrative

**What went wrong:**
- Business model mismatch: real estate (long leases, short-term revenue)
- Governance failure — board couldn't challenge CEO
- Self-dealing and conflicts of interest ( Neumann's related-party deals)
- Cash flow mismatch: long leases, short-term revenue
- Narrative didn't match unit economics

**What it teaches:**
- Business model must match narrative
- Governance matters — board independence is critical
- Cash flow discipline is non-negotiable
- Don't pretend to be tech when you're real estate

---

### 2. Zenefits

**What happened:** Fastest unicorn to crash, faced regulatory issues, pivoted repeatedly.

**Fatal assumption:**
- Skipping licensing requirements is acceptable
- Growth at any cost

**What went wrong:**
- Regulatory compliance failures ( unlicensed insurance brokers)
- Misleading customers about licensing
- Culture of rule-breaking (drinking culture, fast growth)
- Pivot after pivot — lost original identity

**What it teaches:**
- Compliance is not optional — especially in regulated industries
- Growth without integrity destroys value
- Culture compounds — good or bad
- Sometimes the fastest crash follows the fastest rise

---

### 3. GitHub (Acquisition by Microsoft)

**What happened:** Built developer platform, acquired for $7.5B. A "success" but instructive.

**Why it's instructive (not a failure per se):**
- Would have been more valuable as independent? Maybe, maybe not
- Microsoft paid 25x revenue — seemed high at time, looks cheap now
- Key insight: founder-led was critical; Microsoft kept it mostly independent

**Fatal assumption (that competitors make):**
- You can replicate GitHub by adding features
- Network effects can be competed away with resources

**What it teaches:**
- Network effects create durable moats
- Sometimes acquisition is the right exit
- Platform lock-in is real in developer tools

---

### 4. Atlassian (Instructive Near-Failure)

**What happened:** Went from near-death to $60B+ — instructive on survival.

**What almost killed them:**
- 2010: Almost ran out of cash, 6 weeks from bankruptcy
- Wrong pivot: tried to be consulting company
- Lesson: Stick to product

**What saved them:**
- Returned to product (Jira + Confluence)
- Self-serve /PLG before it was popular
- Bootstrapped initially, then raised strategically

**Fatal assumption (that others make):**
- You need massive funding to win
- Product and services can coexist

**What it teaches:**
- Running out of cash is the #1 killer
- Product focus > services
- PLG can work at enterprise scale
- Sometimes less funding forces discipline

---

### 5. Juicero

**What happened:** Raised $120M, shut down. Became symbol of startup excess.

**Fatal assumption:**
- People would pay $400 for a juice press
- Tech-washing hardware would justify valuation
- Premium pricing assumes premium value

**What went wrong:**
- No market need — $400 press vs. $20 manual press
- Over-engineered solution to simple problem
- The press was only necessary for their pre-packaged pouches
- YouTube video: squeezing by hand = squeeze by machine

**What it teaches:**
- Problem-solution fit matters more than technology
- Don't over-engineer simple problems
- Valuation ≠ value creation
- Just because you can build doesn't mean you should

## Productized Services: Additional Cases

### Success: Zapier

**What happened:** No-code automation, $5B valuation.

**Core insight:** Productized the service of "connecting apps" — what consultants charged $10K to do, Zapier does automatically.

**Teaches:** Automating a service = productized service. Value = time saved × frequency.

---

### Success: HubSpot (mentioned above)

**Productized service angle:** They didn't just build software — they productized the methodology of "inbound marketing." The service (getting found online) became the product.

---

### Instructive: Automated Legal Services (LegalZoom, etc.)

**What happened:** Productized legal document creation.

**Insight:** 
- High margin once built
- But: outcomes are hard to guarantee
- Limited expansion beyond documents

**Teaches:** Productized services work best when outcome is measurable and consistent.

---

## Cross-Cutting Lessons

| Success Factors | Failure Factors |
|----------------|----------------|
| Clear market need | No market need |
| Unit economics first | Growth at any cost |
| Workflow integration | Feature bloat |
| Defensible moat | No competitive barrier |
| Governance + oversight | Board/leadership failure |
| Cash flow discipline | Cash flow mismatch |
| Product quality | Product problems masked by sales |
| Network effects | Ignoring compounding |
